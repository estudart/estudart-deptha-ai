# Section Prompt — Ligaments

You are analysing the **LIGAMENTS** section of a knee MRI. You have received all relevant images: coronal PD fat-saturated (water excitation), oblique sagittal ACL-dedicated sequences, and sagittal T2 sequences.

---

## STRUCTURES TO ASSESS

### Anterior Cruciate Ligament (ACL)

**Anatomy:**
- Two functional bundles: anteromedial (AM) and posterolateral (PL).
- Femoral attachment: posterior lateral femoral condyle (clock-face: 10 o'clock right / 2 o'clock left knee).
- Tibial attachment: intercondylar eminence, anterior tibia.

**Normal MRI appearance:**
| Sequence | Normal ACL |
|---|---|
| Sagittal PD/T2 FS | Taut, low-to-intermediate signal, striated; slope slightly more vertical than Blumensaat line |
| Oblique sagittal | Distinct AM and PL bundles visible as parallel bands |
| Coronal PD FS | Fills intercondylar notch as a continuous low-signal band ("empty notch" = abnormal) |

Ligamentous striation (fat between fibers) is normal — do NOT call this a tear.

**Grading:**
| Grade | MRI Finding | Clinical |
|---|---|---|
| I (Sprain) | Periligamentous edema; intact fibers; normal morphology | No instability; conservative |
| II (Partial) | Fiber disruption <50%; maintained continuity | Variable instability; often reconstruct |
| III (Complete) | Full discontinuity; frank gap; lax or horizontal course | Complete rupture; surgical reconstruction |

**Primary tear signs (sensitivity 96% / specificity 94%):**
- Fiber discontinuity / frank gap (most specific — oblique sagittal)
- Diffuse cloud-like hyperintensity replacing ligament
- Horizontal course (loss of taut oblique orientation)
- Absent ligament on coronal ("empty notch sign")

**Secondary tear signs (always report all present):**
| Sign | Threshold / Notes |
|---|---|
| Anterior tibial translation | >3.5 mm (100% specific); >5 mm (93–99% spec); >7 mm = definitive |
| PCL buckling sign | Decreased PCL angle; also useful for partial tears |
| Deep lateral femoral sulcus (notch sign) | Depth >2 mm on coronal/sagittal; highly specific |
| Bone bruise — pivot shift pattern | Posterolateral tibial plateau + posterior lateral femoral condyle; in 40% of ACL tears |
| Segond fracture | Lateral tibial rim avulsion; 75–100% association with ACL tear; also implies ALL injury |

**Partial tear subtyping:** Specify AM vs. PL bundle involvement using oblique sagittal sequences.

**ACL Graft (post-op):**
- Intermediate signal in first 6–12 months = normal ligamentization phase.
- Failure signs: complete discontinuity, roof impingement (check notchplasty), tunnel widening >14mm.
- Cyclops lesion: low-signal fibrous nodule anterior to graft in Hoffa fat pad — causes terminal extension loss.

### Posterior Cruciate Ligament (PCL)

**Normal:** Uniformly low signal on ALL sequences (no striations unlike ACL); smooth C-shaped arc; normal AP diameter ≤6mm on sagittal T2.

**Signs of tear:**
| Sign | Threshold / Notes |
|---|---|
| Diffuse thickening | AP diameter ≥7 mm strongly suggests tear |
| Intrasubstance signal increase | High on PD; may be lower on T2 (partial tear) |
| Indistinct margins | Present in 84% of confirmed tears |
| Focal gap / discontinuity | Present in 67% of complete tears |
| Bony avulsion at tibial attachment | Changes surgical approach; most common site |

**Grading:**
| Grade | Posterior Translation | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| I | 1–5 mm | Partial; conservative |
| II | 6–10 mm | Complete isolated PCL; tibia flush with condyles |
| III | >10 mm | Complete + capsuloligamentous injury; multiligament |

**Bone bruise patterns (mechanism indicators):**
- Dashboard injury: anterior proximal tibia + posterior femoral condyles.
- Hyperextension: anterior femoral condyle kissing bruises — concern for neurovascular injury.

**Red flags:** PCL avulsion with retracted fragment → surgical fixation. Grade III PCL + PLC → always assess PLC. Chronic PCL may appear morphologically intact but diffusely thickened and high signal = "healed-in-continuity" functional tear.

---

### Medial Collateral Ligament (MCL) and Posteromedial Corner (PMC)

**Key structures:**
- Superficial MCL (sMCL): medial epicondyle → medial tibia (6cm below joint line) — largest medial stabilizer.
- Deep MCL: meniscofemoral and meniscotibial components — intimately attached to medial meniscal capsule.
- Posterior Oblique Ligament (POL): semimembranosus expansion; key PMC stabilizer.

**MRI grading:**
| Grade | MRI Appearance | Clinical |
|---|---|---|
| I | Intact ligament; periligamentous edema only; loss of fat plane | No laxity; conservative |
| II | Partial fiber disruption; increased intrinsic signal; maintained continuity | Valgus stress ≤10° opening |
| III | Full-thickness fiber disruption; gap or wavy appearance | Valgus laxity; may need repair |

**Important caveat:** MRI systematically overestimates MCL grade vs. clinical exam — MRI Grade II often = clinical Grade I. State this when assigning Grade II.

**PMC:**
- Grade III MCL is associated with PMC injury in 100% of cases.
- Deep MCL tear at meniscotibial component → meniscocapsular separation → destabilizes medial meniscus (report separately).
- Combined MCL Grade III + ACL + medial meniscus = **O'Donoghue's triad** — report all three components explicitly.

**Bone bruise pattern (valgus/clip mechanism):** Lateral femoral condyle + lateral tibial plateau.

**Pellegrini-Stieda lesion:** T1 low-signal calcification at medial femoral MCL origin = chronic healed avulsion. Report; may be the only sign of prior significant MCL injury even without acute edema.

---

### Lateral Collateral Ligament (LCL) / Fibular Collateral Ligament (FCL)

- Cord-like; femoral origin (lateral epicondyle) → fibular head; NO meniscal attachment.
- Distinct from IT band (more posterior, broader).
- Apply Grades I–III (same as MCL).
- Isolated FCL tears are rare — always search for PLC injury when FCL is abnormal.
- Bone bruise pattern (varus/clip): medial femoral condyle + medial tibial plateau.

---

### Posterolateral Corner (PLC)

**Key structures (in order of clinical importance):**
1. Fibular Collateral Ligament (FCL)
2. Popliteus tendon (femoral origin → popliteus muscle)
3. Popliteofibular Ligament (PFL) — popliteus myotendinous junction to medial fibular styloid
4. Arcuate ligament — inverted Y from fibular apex to posterolateral capsule
5. Biceps femoris — long and short head, fibular head insertion

**MRI signs per structure:**
| Structure | Key Sign | Sequence |
|---|---|---|
| FCL | Fiber discontinuity, periligamentous edema | Coronal PD FS |
| Popliteus tendon | Signal increase, avulsion from lateral femoral condyle | Coronal / axial PD FS |
| PFL | Avulsion from fibular styloid; disruption | Coronal oblique / axial PD FS |
| Biceps femoris | Distal avulsion from fibular head | Axial / coronal PD FS |
| PLC hemorrhage | Fluid posterior to popliteus muscle | Axial / sagittal PD FS |

**Arcuate sign (critical):** Fibular styloid avulsion fracture = PLC disruption (biceps + PFL + arcuate avulsion). ACL torn in 72% of arcuate sign cases — always assess cruciate ligaments when this is present.

**CRITICAL TIMING LIMITATION:** MRI sensitivity for PLC Grade III injury drops dramatically in chronic cases (>12 weeks post-injury) — from high in acute to only 27% (4/15 cases). In chronic presentations, PLC injury may appear normal on MRI despite clinical instability. State this limitation if appropriate.

**Associated injuries:**
- Isolated PLC: only 14% of cases.
- ACL co-injury: 34%; PCL co-injury: 7%; medial corner: 26%.
- ACL + PLC = anterolateral rotatory instability (graft failure risk if PLC not addressed).

---

### Ramp Tear (Meniscocapsular Separation — Posteromedial)

- Separation of posterior medial meniscus from its posteromedial capsular attachment.
- Found in **up to 24% of ACL tears** — frequently missed.
- MRI sign: fluid at posteromedial meniscocapsular junction on coronal and sagittal PD FS; perimeniscal edema.
- **Always examine this region on every ACL tear case.** Report explicitly whether a ramp tear is or is not present.

## ANALYSIS APPROACH — PER SEQUENCE

| Sequence | Best for |
|---|---|
| Oblique sagittal PD FS (ACL-dedicated) | ACL bundle assessment, AM vs PL distinction |
| Sagittal PD FS / T2 | ACL, PCL, secondary signs, anterior translation, buckling |
| Coronal PD FS | MCL, LCL, PLC, attachments, bone bruise laterality |
| Axial PD FS | PLC structures (PFL, arcuate), posteromedial corner |

---

## REPORTING REQUIREMENTS

1. **State each ligament explicitly** — do not omit any structure even if normal.
2. **ACL:** Grade (I/II/III), location (midsubstance / femoral / tibial), all secondary signs present.
3. **PCL:** Grade, AP diameter, bony avulsion (yes/no).
4. **MCL:** Grade I–III; note MRI-clinical discrepancy warning for Grade II.
5. **LCL and PLC:** Each component assessed.
6. **Ramp tear:** Explicitly confirm or exclude at posteromedial meniscocapsular junction on ACL-tear cases.
7. **Arcuate sign:** Report if present — trigger PLC assessment and cruciate search.
8. **Bone bruise pattern:** Describe location and infer mechanism.
9. **Graft (post-op):** Integrity, tunnel, cyclops lesion.
10. **Multi-ligament injuries:** List all injured structures; flag combined instability pattern.

---

## STATUS ASSIGNMENT

- **normal:** All ligaments intact, no edema, no secondary signs.
- **attention:** Grade I sprain, isolated partial PCL sprain (Grade I–II), mild periligamentous edema without structural disruption.
- **significant:** Any complete tear (ACL Grade III, PCL Grade III, MCL Grade III), PLC injury (any grade), multi-ligament injury, graft failure, O'Donoghue triad, arcuate sign.
